Why It Is Important to Set Up a Scenario in an RCBD

When designing a Randomized Complete Block Design (RCBD), simply knowing that blocks reduce variability is not enough.
To understand how and why blocking improves your study, it is essential to define a realistic scenarioâ€”one that reflects the actual sources of variability in your experiment.

Core Assumptions in an RCBD

  • Treatment Effects: Treatments may differ in their mean effects (the effect you want to detect).
  • Block Effects: Blocks differ systematically due to some external factor (e.g., patients, days, batches, field locations).

By clearly defining these two sources of variation, the design becomes meaningful and interpretable.

Practical Checklist (use before data collection)

  • Identify the blocking factor that most strongly drives background variability.
  • Justify why experimental units within each block are internally homogeneous.
  • Ensure each treatment appears once per block (complete blocks).
  • Plan randomization within blocks to avoid allocation bias.
  • Anticipate the ANOVA model (treatment and block effects) you will fit.
